THE ROYAL GAME

Welcome to the fascinating world of chess—a game of strategy, intellect, and infinite possibilities. Whether you are a curious beginner or an aspiring master, there is always something new to discover.

CHESS HISTORY

Chess originated in India during the 6th century as Chaturanga, representing military divisions. It traveled through Persia (where the term "Checkmate" was coined) before evolving in Europe into the game we know today.

  • Global Journey: After India, it spread to the Arab world and then to Latin Europe.
  • Standardization: Modern rules emerged in the late 15th century and gained universal acceptance by the late 19th century.
  • Governance: FIDE (founded in 1924) governs all international competition.

BOARD & STANDARDS

The Staunton Standard

The Staunton pattern, registered in 1849 by Nathaniel Cooke and endorsed by Howard Staunton, is the global standard for all professional tournaments due to its clarity and functionality.

The Grid

Played on an 8x8 board of 64 squares alternating in light and dark colors. Orientation: Always ensure the light square is at the bottom-right corner ("White on the right").

PIECES & RELATIVE VALUES

While every game's goal is to protect the King, knowing the relative point values of your pieces helps you make better trades.

Piece Symbol Point Value Role
Pawn 1 pt The "soul of chess." Used for structure and promotion.
Knight 3 pts The trickster. Jumps over pieces. Deadly in forks.
Bishop 3 pts Long-range diagonal power. Strong in open positions.
Rook 5 pts Heavy artillery. Dominates horizontal/vertical lines.
Queen 9 pts Most powerful piece. Combines Rook and Bishop.
King ∞ The objective. Must be kept safe at all costs.

HOW TO PLAY CHESS

White moves first, after which players alternate turns. One piece is moved per turn.

  • Capture: Pieces are removed by moving your piece to their square (except En Passant).
  • Leaping: The Knight is the only piece that can jump over other pieces.
  • Pawn Capture: Pawns move forward but capture diagonally.

Game Phases

Opening: Developing pieces and controlling the center.
Middlegame: Strategy and planning.
Endgame: Pawn promotion and King activity.

SPECIAL RULES

Castling

Moves King 2 squares toward a Rook to secure safety. Both pieces must be unmoved.

En Passant

Capturing a pawn "in passing" right after it makes a 2-square initial move.

Promotion

Pawn reaching the 8th rank transforms into any major piece (usually a Queen).

FAMOUS OPENINGS

Sicilian Defense
1. e4 c5. The most high-scoring and complex response for Black against White's King Pawn Opening.
Ruy Lopez
1. e4 e5 2. Nf3 Nc6 3. Bb5. Named after a Spanish priest, it is one of the oldest and most studied.
Queen's Gambit
1. d4 d5 2. c4. White offers a pawn to control the center. Popularized globally by recent media.
King's Indian
A "hypermodern" defense where Black lets White take the center to launch a later counterattack.

TACTICAL GLOSSARY

Tactics are short-term maneuvers to gain material or checkmate.

  • The Fork: Attacking two or more enemy pieces simultaneously with one piece.
  • The Pin: Restricting a piece from moving because it would expose a more valuable piece behind it.
  • The Skewer: Attacking a valuable piece, forcing it to move and expose a piece behind it.
  • Discovered Attack: Moving a piece to reveal an attack from a different piece behind it.

WINS & DRAWS

Winning

Checkmate: King trapped in check.
Resignation: Opponent concedes.
Time Win: Opponent's clock runs out.
Forfeit: Violation of rules.

Drawing

Stalemate: No legal moves, not in check.
Threefold Repetition: Same position occurs 3 times.
50-Move Rule: No capture/pawn move in 50 turns.
Dead Position: Impossible to deliver mate.

CHESS ETIQUETTE

Good sportsmanship is essential in the Royal Game.

  • Handshake: Standard practice to shake hands before and after a match.
  • Touch-Move: If you touch a piece, you must move it (if legal).
  • "J'adoube" (I Adjust): Say this *before* touching a piece to adjust its position on the square.
  • Silence: Maintain absolute quiet to respect the opponent's concentration.

THE IMMORTAL GAME (1851)

Played between Adolf Anderssen and Lionel Kieseritzky, this game is the summit of "Romantic Era" chess.

Anderssen sacrificed his Queen, both Rooks, and a Bishop to force a stunning checkmate with just three minor pieces. It remains the ultimate example of tactical beauty over material count.

MODERN CHESS & AI

Chess has moved from quiet libraries to the digital age. In 1997, Deep Blue became the first computer to beat a reigning World Champion. Today, advanced AI like Stockfish and AlphaZero have revolutionized training, while streaming on platforms like Twitch has turned chess into a massive global spectator sport.
