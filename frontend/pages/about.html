THE ESSENCE OF CHESS

An abstract strategy mind sport for two players, played on a 64-square grid. The ultimate goal: Checkmate the enemy King.

HISTORY & WORLD CHAMPIONS

Chess originated from Chaturanga in 7th-century India, spreading through Persia and the Arab world before reaching Europe.

  • Evolution: Modern rules were standardized in Europe by the late 15th century.
  • Governance: The International Chess Federation (FIDE) governs global competitions.
  • First Champion: Wilhelm Steinitz (1886).
  • Current Champion: Gukesh Dommaraju (won in 2024).

THE BOARD & PIECES

Setup Orientation

Place the board so a light square is at the bottom-right ("White on right"). The Queen starts on her own color square.

Pieces (16 each)

1 King, 1 Queen, 2 Rooks, 2 Bishops, 2 Knights, and 8 Pawns. The Staunton pattern is the standard for competitions.

HOW PIECES MOVE

Piece Movement Pattern
King One square in any direction. Most valuable piece.
Queen Any number of squares vertically, horizontally, or diagonally.
Rook Any number of squares horizontally or vertically.
Bishop Any number of squares diagonally.
Knight "L-shape" (2x1). The only piece that can leap over others.
Pawn Forward one square (two on first move). Captures diagonally.

SPECIAL MOVES

Castling: King moves two squares toward a Rook, and the Rook hops over him. Only possible if neither has moved and no pieces are between them.
En Passant: A special pawn capture immediately after an opponent's pawn makes a two-square advance.
Promotion: A pawn reaching the 8th rank is replaced by a Queen, Rook, Bishop, or Knight.

CHECK & CHECKMATE

Check: When a King is under immediate attack. You must capture the attacker, block the attack, or move the King.

Checkmate: The King is in check and there is no legal way to escape. This ends the game with a win for the attacker.

HOW GAMES END

Winning

  • Checkmate
  • Resignation
  • Win on Time (Clock runs out)
  • Forfeit (Rules violations)

Drawing

  • Stalemate (No legal moves, not in check)
  • Dead Position (Impossible to mate)
  • Mutual Agreement
  • Threefold Repetition
  • Fifty-Move Rule

TIME & NOTATION

Competitions use Chess Clocks. Categories include Classical (long), Rapid, Blitz, and Bullet (under 3 mins).

Algebraic Notation: The standard for recording moves (e.g., e4, Nf3, Bxf3+). White moves are recorded first, followed by Black.

THEORY & PHASES

  • Opening: Focus on development, center control, and king safety.
  • Middlegame: Strategic planning and tactical combinations (Forks, Pins, Skewers).
  • Endgame: Few pieces remain; Pawns and Kings become much more powerful.

CHESS & TECHNOLOGY

In 1997, Deep Blue became the first computer to beat a World Champion (Garry Kasparov). Today, engines like Stockfish and AlphaZero are significantly stronger than any human and have revolutionized chess theory.
